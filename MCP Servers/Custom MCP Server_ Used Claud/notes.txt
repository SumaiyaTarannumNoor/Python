1. An existential crisis is a period of intense questioning about the meaning, purpose, and value of one's life — often triggered by major life events like loss, failure, or milestone birthdays.
2. The concept was popularized by existentialist philosophers like Sartre, Camus, and Kierkegaard, who argued that humans must create their own meaning in an indifferent universe.
3. Common triggers include: the death of a loved one, divorce, job loss, a serious illness, turning a significant age, or feeling "stuck" in life with no sense of direction.
4. Symptoms can include persistent feelings of emptiness, anxiety, loss of motivation, questioning one's identity, and a sense that nothing truly matters.
5. Albert Camus described the "absurd" — the conflict between humans' desire for meaning and the universe's silence on the matter. His solution: embrace life anyway (absurdism).
6. An existential crisis, while painful, can be a catalyst for profound personal growth — forcing individuals to re-evaluate their values, relationships, and goals with fresh clarity.
7. Viktor Frankl, Holocaust survivor and psychiatrist, argued in "Man's Search for Meaning" that the primary human drive is not pleasure or power, but the search for meaning — even in suffering.
8. Existential crises are not a sign of weakness or mental illness — they are a deeply human experience. However, if they lead to prolonged depression or hopelessness, professional support is important.
9. Practical ways to navigate an existential crisis: journaling, mindfulness, connecting with community, exploring philosophy or spirituality, and talking to a therapist or trusted person.
10. Ultimately, an existential crisis invites us to ask: "What truly matters to me?" Answering that question honestly — however uncomfortable — is the beginning of a more intentional and meaningful life.
